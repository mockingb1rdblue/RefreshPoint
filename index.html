Paid Sick Leave: Healthy Families and Workplaces Act (HFWA)

The Colorado Healthy Families and Workplaces Act (HFWA) is the primary law governing paid sick leave in Colorado. It requires employers to provide paid sick leave to their employees. This law was fully implemented for all employers as of January 1, 2022.

This page answers common questions about your rights under HFWA.

Understanding Your HFWA Rights

The Colorado Department of Labor and Employment (CDLE) provides detailed information about HFWA.

  • CDLE Page on Wage & Hour Laws (includes HFWA): CDLE: Wage & Hour Laws (including Paid Sick Leave)
  • CDLE Interpretive Notice & Formal Opinion (“INFO”) #6B (Overview of HFWA): This document offers a comprehensive overview of paid sick leave under HFWA. View INFO #6B
  • Full Text of the Act: For the specific statutory language. (See section 8-13.3-410 for "Retaliatory personnel action prohibited"). View Full Text of HFWA

No, your employer cannot retaliate against you for using your accrued paid sick leave. This includes disciplinary actions like marking you with an attendance occurrence or "point," demoting you, reducing your hours, or terminating your employment.

HFWA explicitly prohibits employers from discriminating or retaliating against an employee for requesting or using paid sick leave. If you believe you've been retaliated against, you have the right to file a complaint. (See "Retaliation Under Colorado Law" section below for more details).

If your employer provides a general Paid Time Off (PTO) policy that meets or exceeds the HFWA requirements for accrual and use, they don't have to provide separate sick leave.

This means your PTO can be used for any reason, including sickness, without needing to specify it's "sick leave." Crucially, you are able to use this PTO any way you want. The employer cannot restrict your use of this PTO for sick reasons if it's meant to cover HFWA requirements, nor can they penalize you for using it for HFWA-qualifying reasons.

The PTO policy must allow you to accrue and use leave at least as generously as HFWA (1 hour per 30 hours worked, up to 48 hours per year, usable as accrued).

Under HFWA, you accrue paid sick leave (up to 48 hours per year) regardless of other PTO balances. If you have accrued HFWA sick leave, you are entitled to use it for qualifying reasons even if you have exhausted any other PTO (like vacation time) provided by your employer.

You are allowed to use your accrued HFWA sick days without retaliation, even if you have no other PTO remaining. Your employer must allow you to use the sick leave you have earned under HFWA.

Key Features of HFWA

  • Accrual Rate: 1 hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked.
  • Annual Cap: Employees can use up to 48 hours of paid sick leave per year. They can also carry over up to 48 hours of unused leave to the next year, but employers are not required to allow use of more than 48 hours in a single year.
  • Covered Uses:
    • Your own mental or physical illness, injury, or health condition; preventive medical care.
    • Caring for a family member's mental or physical illness, injury, or health condition; or their preventive medical care.
    • Needs due to domestic abuse, sexual assault, or criminal harassment (for medical care, mental health care, legal services, victim services, or relocation).
    • When a public health emergency closes your workplace or your child's school/place of care.
    • For more examples, see the Physical & Mental Health Conditions page.
  • No Retaliation: Employers cannot punish, discipline, or fire employees for using their HFWA leave.

Retaliation Under Colorado Law

Colorado law protects employees from retaliation for exercising their rights under various labor laws, including HFWA.

This CDLE document explains what constitutes illegal retaliation or interference, what employee actions are protected (like requesting HFWA leave), and what employer actions are considered "adverse."

Key points from INFO #5A regarding HFWA:

  • Protected Activity Includes: Requesting or taking paid leave under HFWA, informing others about HFWA rights, or filing an HFWA complaint.
  • Adverse Actions Include: Termination, demotion, reduction in hours/pay, discipline (such as unwarranted warnings), threats, harassment, or any action that would dissuade a reasonable employee from exercising their rights.

Important Note on Discipline:

Marking an attendance occurrence/point for using protected leave is considered an adverse action and a form of prohibited retaliation under HFWA.

Colorado Family and Medical Leave Insurance (FAMLI) Program

CO FAMLI is a state-run insurance program providing Colorado workers with paid leave for significant life events. It helps ensure you don't have to choose between your job and your health or family. This is an earned benefit, not welfare.

FAMLI provides partial wage replacement during leave.

  • Paid Time Off: Receive a portion of your wages.
  • Job Protection: Generally, if you've worked for your employer for at least 180 days.
  • Comprehensive Coverage: For your own serious health (physical or mental), family care, new child bonding, military deployment needs, and safe leave from domestic violence. (See Physical & Mental Health Conditions page for more).
  • Peace of Mind: Focus on health/family without total income loss.
  • Reduces Stigma: Normalizes taking leave for life events, including mental health.

Funding: Through premiums (0.9% of wages in 2024), typically split 50/50 between employee (0.45%) and employer (0.45%). Employers with <10 employees don't pay the employer share but remit the employee's.

Eligibility: Most CO employees who've earned at least $2,500 in wages (subject to FAMLI premiums) in their "base period" (first 4 of last 5 calendar quarters). This includes full-time, part-time, and seasonal workers. Self-employed can opt-in.

Benefits are on a sliding scale based on your average weekly wage vs. the state average:

  • 90% of your wage portion that is 50% or less of the state average.
  • 50% of your wage portion that is more than 50% of the state average.

Max weekly benefit in 2024 was $1,100 (adjusted annually). Benefits are taxable; you can opt for withholding.

FAMLI covers leave for:

  • Your Own Serious Health Condition: Physical or mental, including conditions like depression, anxiety, PTSD if certified by a healthcare provider.
  • Caring for a Family Member with a Serious Health Condition.
  • Bonding with a New Child.
  • Military Exigencies.
  • Safe Leave (related to domestic violence, stalking, etc.).

For detailed examples, especially concerning mental health and workplace stress, please see the Physical & Mental Health Conditions page.

General Steps to Start a FAMLI Claim:

  1. Visit the Official FAMLI Website: Go to famli.colorado.gov.
  2. Create or Log In to My FAMLI+: This is the online portal for managing claims.
  3. Start a New Claim: Follow the prompts to begin your application. You'll typically need to provide:
    • Your personal information.
    • Your employer's information.
    • The reason for your leave (e.g., own serious health condition, bonding with new child, caring for family member).
    • The type of leave you're requesting (continuous, intermittent, reduced schedule).
    • Anticipated start and end dates of your leave.
  4. Obtain and Submit Documentation:
    • For your own serious health condition or to care for a family member, you will be provided with a Medical Certification form. You need to have the relevant healthcare provider complete this form. You'll then upload it to your My FAMLI+ account. See the Medical Form Facsimile page for an idea of what this form includes.
    • For bonding leave, you'll typically need proof of birth or placement.
    • Other types of leave will have their own documentation requirements.
  5. Await Determination: The FAMLI Division will review your application and documentation and notify you of their decision.

A Note on Notifying Your Employer:

CO FAMLI generally advises employees to notify their employer of their intent to take leave (usually 30 days in advance if foreseeable, or as soon as practicable otherwise). After this initial notification, you would proceed with your FAMLI application. Once FAMLI reviews your claim and the Medical Certification Form (if required) and approves your leave, you can then provide a more definitive update to your employer with the approved dates and details.

However, some employees may feel more comfortable or secure waiting until their FAMLI claim is officially approved before having a detailed discussion with their employer, especially if they are concerned about potential misunderstandings or negative reactions should the claim be unexpectedly delayed or denied. While you must still provide notice as required, the exact timing of detailed discussions versus initial notification can be a personal consideration based on your workplace environment. You are not required to disclose your specific medical diagnosis to your employer.

You should generally provide your employer with at least 30 days' notice of your intent to take leave if the need is foreseeable. If it's not foreseeable, provide notice as soon as practicable.

Examples of what to tell your employer:

  • "I am planning to apply for CO FAMLI leave for my own serious health condition, starting on [Start Date] and I anticipate returning on [End Date]. I will keep you updated as my application progresses and once I have approval from FAMLI."
  • "I need to request FAMLI leave to care for a family member with a serious health condition. I expect this leave to begin around [Date] and last approximately [Duration]. I will provide more specific dates once confirmed with FAMLI."
  • "I will be taking FAMLI leave for parental bonding starting on [Start Date] for [Number] weeks. I have initiated my claim with FAMLI."

You do not need to provide your employer with your specific medical diagnosis or personal details of a safe leave situation. The FAMLI Division handles the verification of the qualifying reason. Your employer may require you to follow their usual procedures for requesting leave, as long as those procedures don't interfere with your FAMLI rights.

What you are NOT obligated to provide your employer:

  • Your specific medical diagnosis or detailed medical records.
  • Detailed personal information about a situation qualifying for safe leave beyond what is necessary to establish the need for leave under FAMLI.

Up to 12 weeks of paid leave per application year. An additional 4 weeks (total 16) for serious health conditions due to pregnancy/childbirth complications.

Leave can be taken: Continuous (all at once), Intermittent (separate blocks), or as a Reduced Schedule.

Job Protection:

If you've worked for your current employer for at least 180 days before FAMLI leave, you're generally entitled to reinstatement to the same or equivalent position. Employers cannot retaliate for taking FAMLI leave.

For the most accurate and up-to-date information, applications, and FAQs, visit the official CO FAMLI website.

Physical & Mental Health Under Colorado Leave Laws

Both the Healthy Families and Workplaces Act (HFWA) and the CO FAMLI program provide job-protected leave for a variety of physical and mental health needs. Understanding how these apply, especially in stressful work environments, is crucial.

Disclaimer: The DSM-5 and ICD-10-CM codes mentioned below are examples for informational purposes only and do not constitute medical or billing advice. Diagnosis and code selection must be done by qualified healthcare and billing professionals based on individual assessment. Eligibility for leave is determined by the respective state agencies based on official documentation and program rules.

Mental Health, Workplace Stress, and Leave

Challenging work environments, including those with excessive overtime, high pressure, or toxic dynamics, can significantly impact mental health. Both HFWA and FAMLI can offer support if these conditions lead to diagnosable health issues.

HFWA allows you to use your accrued paid sick leave (up to 48 hours/year) for:

  • Your own mental or physical illness, injury, or health condition that prevents work.
  • Obtaining preventive medical care, or medical diagnosis/care/treatment of any mental or physical illness, injury, or health condition.

Examples related to mental health & workplace stress:

  • Taking a day off due to severe anxiety or a panic attack that makes it impossible to work effectively.
  • Attending a therapy appointment for stress, depression, or anxiety.
  • Needing a "mental health day" when symptoms of a diagnosed condition (e.g., depression flare-up) prevent you from working, even if you don't see a doctor that specific day (though documentation rules apply for absences of 4+ consecutive workdays).
  • Recovering from acute stress symptoms (e.g., severe insomnia, inability to concentrate) that temporarily prevent you from performing your job.

Important Note for HFWA: While you can use HFWA for these reasons, if an absence is for 4 or more consecutive workdays, your employer can request reasonable documentation. This doesn't necessarily mean a doctor's note for every instance, especially for shorter absences. (Refer to the Sick Leave page for documentation details).

FAMLI provides longer-term paid leave (up to 12 weeks) for your own "serious health condition." This explicitly includes mental health conditions that incapacitate you from performing essential job functions or require ongoing medical treatment.

Examples related to mental health & workplace stress (requiring healthcare provider certification):

  • Diagnosed Depression or Anxiety Disorders: If severe symptoms (e.g., inability to concentrate, persistent fatigue, panic attacks, social withdrawal) stemming from or exacerbated by a toxic work environment or chronic overtime abuse make you unable to work for an extended period.
    (Potential codes: ICD-10-CM F32.x - Major depressive disorder, single episode; F33.x - Major depressive disorder, recurrent; F41.1 - Generalized anxiety disorder; F41.0 - Panic disorder. Diagnosis based on DSM-5 criteria.)
  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): If workplace events (e.g., harassment, witnessing a traumatic incident) lead to PTSD requiring time off for treatment and recovery.
    (Potential codes: ICD-10-CM F43.1 - Post-traumatic stress disorder. Diagnosis based on DSM-5 criteria.)
  • Burnout Leading to a Serious Health Condition: While "burnout" itself isn't a primary medical diagnosis for FAMLI, if chronic workplace stress leads to a clinically diagnosed condition like severe depression, an anxiety disorder, or a stress-related physical illness that meets the "serious health condition" criteria, it could qualify.
    (Potential codes: The underlying diagnosed condition's code, e.g., F32.x/F33.x for depression. Note: ICD-10-CM Z73.0 is for "Burn-out" as a factor influencing health status, not typically a primary qualifying condition for FAMLI itself without a co-occurring serious health condition.)
  • Inpatient or Intensive Outpatient Treatment: Needing time off for admission to a mental health facility or participation in an intensive therapy program for a qualifying serious health condition.
    (Potential codes: Dependent on the underlying condition requiring treatment, e.g., F33.2 for severe recurrent major depression requiring hospitalization.)
  • Adjustment Disorders: If a significant workplace stressor leads to an adjustment disorder with severe symptoms that impair your ability to work and require treatment.
    (Potential codes: ICD-10-CM F43.2x - Adjustment disorders. Diagnosis based on DSM-5 criteria.)

Key for FAMLI: A healthcare provider (doctor, psychologist, licensed therapist, etc.) must certify that you have a serious health condition that prevents you from working or requires ongoing treatment. The focus is on the diagnosed condition and its impact on your ability to work, though the workplace stressors can be relevant information for your provider.

"Lack of motivation" or "feeling overwhelmed" on their own are not typically sufficient for FAMLI unless they are symptoms of an underlying, certified serious health condition.

Physical Health and Leave

HFWA covers shorter-term physical health needs:

  • Common illnesses like the flu, colds, infections.
  • Minor injuries (e.g., a sprain that prevents you from doing your job for a day or two).
  • Preventive care: doctor check-ups, dental appointments, vaccinations.
  • Diagnostic appointments or treatments for an existing condition.
  • Caring for a family member with similar short-term physical health needs.

Example from overtime abuse: Developing repetitive strain injuries (e.g., carpal tunnel, back pain) due to long hours and poor ergonomics, requiring a doctor's visit or a day of rest to manage acute pain.

FAMLI is for "serious health conditions" that are more substantial or require longer recovery:

  • Surgery and recovery.
    (Potential codes: Vary widely based on surgery type, e.g., ICD-10-CM codes from Chapters corresponding to body system, plus Z51.89 - Encounter for other specified aftercare.)
  • Hospitalization (inpatient care).
    (Potential codes: Dependent on reason for hospitalization.)
  • Chronic conditions requiring ongoing treatment or causing episodic incapacity (e.g., severe migraines - G43.x, autoimmune flare-ups - M30-M36 range).
  • Serious injuries requiring extended recovery.
  • Pregnancy-related serious health conditions (including prenatal care - Z34.x, recovery from childbirth - Z39.x, and complications - O00-O9A range).
  • Caring for a family member with a serious physical health condition.

Example from overtime abuse: A stress-induced physical condition like severe hypertension (I10) or a heart condition (e.g., I20-I25 for ischemic heart diseases) exacerbated by chronic overwork, requiring extended medical leave for stabilization and treatment, as certified by a doctor.

Remember: For FAMLI, a healthcare provider must certify the serious health condition.

Important Considerations

  • Documentation is Key: Especially for FAMLI, and for HFWA leave of 4+ consecutive workdays, medical certification or appropriate documentation is generally required. Be proactive in communicating with your healthcare provider.
  • Employer Policies: Familiarize yourself with your employer's specific policies for requesting leave, but remember these policies cannot infringe upon your rights under HFWA or FAMLI.
  • Seek Clarity: If unsure, refer to the official CDLE and FAMLI Colorado websites, or consult with an employment law professional.
